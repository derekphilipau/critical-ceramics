Colby Parsons OKeefe posted 01-02-2004 09:26 PM ET (US)            
To focus on the digital part of the discussion, I propose dividing the effects of technology on artistic practice into three main areas of influence: direct technical influence, direct content influence, and indirect influence.

Direct technical influence involves technical options that have expanded our range of expression. In some cases new technology makes it possible to do things we have never done before, such as using Computer Aided Design modeling and 3-D �printing� to create precisely engineered forms. In other cases, improvements in technology have lowered the price of a certain process drastically (or offered a similar way to get the same result), making it far more accessible. An example of this is Xerox or inkjet transfer. Another direct influence is the use of digital technology itself in a multimedia piece; for example, using video or sound (both of which could have been used previously, but have become far easier to record, edit and play back).

Direct content influence involves artwork made as an explicit commentary on living in a �computer age�, or when the content seems clearly derived from the concerns of a digital culture. This can show up as sculptures of computers or cell-phones, or the incorporation of images or patterns derived from digital technology. Faux-ancient pottery with anachronistic imagery as part of the decoration is an excellent example. Also, a great deal of the text used in artwork can be seen as the product of an information-saturated society, especially when its presentation derives from advertising layouts.

Indirect influence involves the ways in which digital technology has changed our approaches or modes of thought, and that in turn has affected our artwork. To me this is the most evasive aspect to define, and also the most interesting. I say �evasive� because the types of influence I am talking about have become so commonplace as to fade into the background. The internet is probably the most obvious indirect influence � making it easy to communicate with other artists or find information relating to our field. Such communication has made it possible to be part of a much larger community of artists if we choose to be. I suspect, however, that there are less obvious influences. I recall a moment several years ago after I had just gotten familiar with a computer graphics program, when I made a mistake on a clay sculpture and I had a split-second urge to press UNDO. The possibility of a different kind of revision had crept into my thinking. Now I�m not saying for certain that such a change affected my work, but I�m not sure if I could identify it if it had. That is the sort of indirect influence I am enquiring into. If you have any thoughts on the matter, please share them.

As for the three categories mentioned (Direct Technical, Direct Content and Indirect) I think the internet plays a principal role, transcending each of the categories.

Direct Technical: The internet provides an opportunity for easy exchange of information, materials and technology, making processes and new technology more viable to artists regardless of location.

Direct Content: The internet is largely responsible for the over saturation of information in our current society, which is likely to surface in any artists� work that addresses our current culture. Because it has become so commonplace, as Colby mentioned, it is hard to pinpoint how pervasive that influence might be. I know that I have seen some of Fred�s work directly addresses the internet. Other artists� work is probably influenced by the internet, maybe in less obvious or conscious ways, but being an artist living in this time, it is almost impossible to ignore.

Indirect Influence: Besides creating an incredible communication network (evident in the fact that we are able to carry on this discussion), the internet has created an opportunity for a virtual gallery of numerous artists, providing exposure beyond what traditional galleries and printed publications can accomplish. However, these virtual images are usually low quality and certainly do not replace the experience of viewing the actual object in a gallery or even a high resolution photograph in a printed publication. This raises some interesting questions about the virtual vs the actual world that I think tie in to what Brian said �digital communication has introduced a level of dematerialation of physical experience to a point of the predictable over reaction which rejects technology and longs for a more simple time.�

Colby Parsons OKeefe posted 01-03-2004 11:41 PM ET (US)            
This is a draft of my presentation for NCECA:(some things will definitely be changed based on the fact that I am describing things that will not need to be described in the lecture, since they will be shown on the screen.)


I want to present a group of my works that fits perfectly into this conversation about digital and elemental influences. I have been creating mixed media works that combine fired clay with a video image of that fired clay. In essence showing two versions of the same thing, but using very different mediums.
Each of these pieces incorporates a series of small clay sculptures that are designed like cells in an animation film, with minor sequential adjustments from one to the next. Displayed alongside the sculptures is a video made using images of these sculptures. The video, which loops indefinitely, seems to show a simple object involved in a simple motion: growing then receding, rolling left then rolling right, melting then re-forming, etc.
Part of the point of these artworks is to give a sense of play. In making them I let myself play, and that is part of what I want viewers to feel when they see the work.
The other thing I was after, that more directly relates to this discussion, is that I wanted to take some of the sense of movement and plasticity that is there when a piece is being created, and have that kind of energy still there in the finished work. I was looking at how, particularly in the last hundred years, artists have tried in many ways to give a sense of movement or life to immobile paintings and sculpture. And while the strategies have often been effective, they generally give a sort of �snapshot� sense of life or motion, rather than strategies that match our experience of actual motion.
Now you could say that this work is an exploration of how motion can be expressed artistically in an �elemental� format (clay) versus how it could be expressed in a �digital� format (video). And the results say a lot to me about the wonderful possibilities within each format, as well as the distinct limits. So I�m going to juxtapose these two mediums as a way to discuss those limits and possibilities.
First there is the clay, with its myriad of associations. It is elemental in the sense of it�s being tactile, earthy and solid, and for me it also has a fleshy connotation. It is an ancient medium, having been used by humans to create imagined forms for at least the past 30,000 years. It is also analog (like any object that is actual, rather than virtual) and has an infinite level of detail. The closer you get to inspect it, the more you see. Clay has qualities of depth and texture and ways that it reflects or gathers light that cannot be reliably communicated; and some of which may not even be apparent to someone new to the field. Elemental suggests something raw and rugged to me, but it also suggests the power of a pure material, like a quartz crystal. It is physical, and as such can never be precisely duplicated (although a facsimile could be made almost identical, in reality each copy would have unique qualities at some level). Clay is also fully dimensional, meaning that as you move around it, there are subtle changes in what you actually see; different perspectives, different highlights and shadows. On the other hand, a limitation of the ceramic part of these works is that they are immobile. Though they can be formed in a way that continues to express a sense of plasticity and movement after they are fired, the fact remains that fired clay cannot be bent or reshaped without breaking it.
Secondly, there is the video, representing digital influences partly because the video I am using IS digital, but also in a broader sense in that it consists of encoded information processed electronically. Video is clearly very different from clay as a way to present something visually. It is flat; allowing only one perspective for viewing. It is limited in terms of detail (the closer you get to the image, the less detail you see). Because it is digital, identical copies can be easily and precisely reproduced without limit, and the ten thousandth one would be the same as the first. Compared to clay, video is a tiny infant of a medium. Television sets have only been available for about 75 years, while video as an art form has only existed for about 30 years, and digital video even more briefly still. Far from having an ancient lineage, video is just barely emerging from the �gimmick� stage. It can be flashy and attention-grabbing. But it is also bright and alive with movement. This is the strongest quality of video as far as I am concerned. In my own work, my intention is to use video to give life to inanimate objects. I see this as an act of technological sorcery; bringing a quality of aliveness to objects that is mesmerizing even when one is entirely aware of the illusion involved.
Finally, apart from what each of these mediums brings separately, there is also something that they bring synergistically. One who has only seen the ceramic sculptures has a sense of their suggestion of movement, but cannot visualize it with full clarity. One who has only seen the video portion of the work sees the full movement, but cannot experience the rich detail of the material itself. The synergy is that the viewing of one format infects the perception of the other. Having seen the video, the objects no longer seem as static, but more so as frozen moments. Having seen the objects, the video seems like a magic trick to which you have just learned the secret.

Thanks for your post. Here is my latest revision and the most complete list of questions so far.

For me as with most of us the influence of the elemental precedes that of the digital. In particular clay�s paradoxical qualities of fluid plasticity as in surfing the internet and hard fired structure hold a strong attraction for me. I use the ready-made brick form of clay, a ubiquitous processed commodity, to build in its elemental form. The act of building however is not enough. How does an elemental desire to build form out of material, relate to my experience of the rest of the surrounding environment? What kind of response can I have to the visual complexity of a world where image, form, and text is reformatted and repackaged? What if each brick was a pixel or a bit of information? What if these images processed were built into a structure that retained the potential for reformatting? Would I thereby be creating a brick sculpture that becomes virtual? The answer has come in rebuilding various brick structures with glazed images and text, installed in shifting public, educational, and commercial settings. The structures though sometimes large are built of small particles. Although they have mass and solidity they retain a temporal fluid nature. They are made of commercially made products combined with an earthy ceramic process. They incorporate images like bits of perception and in turn become images themselves. Bricks are gleaned over and over again from previous sculptures for a new site, a new viewing audience.
