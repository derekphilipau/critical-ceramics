My response to this work is that firstly, it is impeccably crafted and it is a convincing representation of its influences and sources. The work is compelling and engaging.

However, upon deeper examination, I do find the work to be somewhat cold and distant; connected as much as an observer would be to any object. My feeling is that these objects are not connected to a specific experience other than the viewing of similar objects in their environment. So for me the question is, how will they function in a gallery setting, if that is where they are intended to be shown? What message are they intended to convey?

For me, this work evokes the memory of my own personal experiences that are most likely similar to those of the artist... memories of viewing gravestones in their environment (i.e.. the graveyard) and creating imaginary narratives about the lives of the persons that are described on the headstones.

I think that a less conventional glaze treatment could serve to push this work in a less conventional direction thusly taking the viewer to place they have not yet been. I believe that the work is already conventional and literal enough in its representation, certainly the surface will serve to reinforce the artistic intent... I think recreating the sense of aged stone is too literal. Rather than recreating what has already been seen, what other options exist in the mind of the maker?

mike posted 12-04-2003 06:56 PM ET (US)            
an interesting concept here is that you are making a sculpture (and a rather literal one at that) of a hand crafted item. there's a great tradition of this in ceramics in the form of non-functional pottery or decorative pottery. there are thousands of examples of pottery forms that have been reduced to sculpture that you might want to take some inspiration from. clay sculptors tackled a problem quite similar to this one when they started making pottery specifically for non-utilitarian usage (display in galleries, etc.). i would assume that one could make a few generalizations regarding qualities found in the surfaces of non-utilitarian pottery that discern it from utilitarian pottery. if you choose to do some research to look at this idea this you might also be able to make some type of commentary about that particular genre of ceramic art.

don't forget - these things already have a surface. just 'cause it's there doesn't mean you got to put glaze on it. if it wasn't an important part of your original idea maybe you should just leave it as it is. and plan the next ones around a more comprehensive idea.

by the way, i think it's critical to have input form your peers during the creative process. thanks for letting us see your unfinished work. it looks great. where can we see it when it's finished?

mike flaherty

Forrest- can you elaborate on the stories you want to tell througn these markers?
Also- I've been thinking about the markers- what about the primary and secondary contours of them? The primary contour can stays the same hard edged geometric shape - but surface or secondary contour can be carved from, stacked tightly with applied or carved objects etc to fill the space and present the viewer with part of the story being told-giving us information about the person being represented by the marker. This could be anything from the tools used by the person, vocation held or other objects from the period in which the person lived. Robert Arneson played with this primary/secondary contour in a show of his about war. He displayed a war missile and from a distance you could see a missile but as you approached the piece it became evident that the missile form was actually a mound of bodies. This was a very powerful image to say the least- and it told a dreadful story.
