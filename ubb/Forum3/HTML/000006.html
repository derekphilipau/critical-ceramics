mike posted 26-05-2003 03:53 PM ET (US)            
none of these function in any way whatsoever, and none of them are "sculptural" pots either. they are purely sculptural. i didn't want to start with a pot and then "dress it up" as something else. i did, however want to allude to the idea of function. as you can see i have done this in several ways. the mechanical aesthetic as well as the reference to pottery technology and forms accomplish this (i hope!) this is all intended to draw some type of relationship between the idea of function and the other important subject matter in the pieces - the human body.

wheelerg posted 20-09-2003 09:22 PM ET (US)            
Mike,
First of all, I comend you for putting up your work in this forum. It takes a certain type of bravery to expose your self to anonymous criticism.

My confusion in looking at your work is its relationship to your artist statement. You are voicing an interest in social criticism, yet your work doesn't seem to be saying anything very specific. The work reads as toy / robot / soldier, but doesn't seem to be taking any particular stance, or making any real commentary.

Unlike some of the other people critiquing your work, I have no problem seeing the found materials, however from what I can tell, you are using them only as a means of adding a variety of texture and material, they don't add to the narative or commentary at all.

I think you need to ask yourself more specifically what "message" you are trying to get across to the viewer. If these are purely sculptural objects, why is it important to you that you refer to the "craft" of thrown vessels? Why are these even made of clay at all? I don't mean to suggest that they shouldn't be, or that there is no reason to be throwing them, but I think that you need to think it out more clearly and to make a stronger statement, otherwise they end up just being "cute" little chotzkes.

Jiri Lonsky posted 06-12-2003 11:37 PM ET (US)            
The objects are made with precision and do reference the machine. However, I do not see how do they make social commentary, nor how are they specific. For that purpose, to leave them untitled is a great loss, since title alone gives a great opportunity for personal view, commentary, satire, social criticism etc. I do not see how the teapot reference contributes to the professed goal of the body of work, personally it confuses me. The first four are robots, really, the interesting part being their awkwardness of posture, the pot-bellied quality that goes against the grain of what one expects from stereotypical image of a robot as a (potentially) menacing machine. I think scale is a great issue here. At twelve inches, the objects read as a cute, toy-like. Imagine them 7ft tall - another story altogether. The last two are to me the most successful, precisely because they leave the expected and the predictable format. Also, the introduction of color helps a great deal.
