NCECA '99, COLUMBUS, OHIO

Forrest Snyder and Judith Enright report their thoughts from Columbus.


It’s has been nearly two weeks since NCECA 1999 concluded in Columbus. When I get moments to reflect, I recall some awfully meaningful impressions. Theyï¿½re even more powerful now that I’m out of the rush of the event and my cold has cleared up.

NCECA is an amazing happening. Think of it - 3500 kindred spirits assembled in a new city each year to celebrate and lament their passion. Can you imagine painters doing such a thing? Perhaps printmakers could create a similar camaraderie. In the din of the crowd at the Hyatt bar, an attending non-ceramic sculptor friend said, in effect, “I don’t understand it. I love it, but I don’t get it. I want to come back next year!”

The conference isn’t all beer drinking and socializing, however. To be sure, there are serious artists presenting their work, academics sharing their research, students pursuing the quest for knowledge, and critics engaging in intense debate.

If I have a complaint, it is that NCECA has become too popular. Note that I mean popular and not successful. In sheer numbers, the conference is very popular - growing annually in recent years. However, as a scholarly presentation and an artistic exhibition, NCECA has a long way to go.

It’s a good thing that ceramic artists have great senses of humor. Can you imagine another academic field where the premier event of the year consists of presenters who have not rehearsed, audio-video equipment that continuously breaks down, damaged works are featured, and, worse still, the highlighted works are often mediocre? I’ve attended similar conferences on education, medical ethics, and computer science. Their professional standards are much, much higher than those of the National Council on Education for the Ceramics Arts. The goal for each conference must be to be exemplary in every aspect.

I must acknowledge the great efforts of Judith Enright. Judith has pulled through like a trouper and written three stream-of-consciousness pieces for all of us to enjoy. Click on the images on the left to read her words. Thank you Judith!
